How to Measure and Understand Body Composition (BIA) on Samsung Galaxy Watch

For decades, tracking fitness progress relied almost entirely on the bathroom scale. However, body weight alone is a highly flawed metric. A scale cannot distinguish between five pounds of muscle and five pounds of fat, nor can it account for daily shifts in water weight. To truly understand your fitness levels, you need to analyze your body composition—specifically, how much of your body consists of fat, muscle, bone, and water.

With the introduction of the 3-in-1 BioActive Sensor (starting on the Galaxy Watch 4 and continuing through the Galaxy Watch 5, 6, and 7 series), Samsung brought clinical-grade Bioelectrical Impedance Analysis (BIA) directly to the consumer wrist. In this guide, we'll explain how this advanced BIA sensor works, how to get the most accurate readings, and how to interpret your body composition metrics to fine-tune your nutrition and workouts.

What is Bioelectrical Impedance Analysis (BIA)?

BIA is a technique that measures body composition by sending a safe, extremely weak microcurrent through your body. The current travels from the watch's back sensor, up through one arm, across your torso, and down the other arm to the two fingers resting on the physical watch buttons (which act as electrodes).

As the current flows, it encounters different levels of electrical resistance (known as impedance):

  • Muscle, water, and blood contain high levels of water and electrolytes, allowing the current to pass through quickly with low resistance.
  • Fat tissue contains very little water, acting as an insulator and offering high resistance to the electrical signal.

By measuring the voltage drop and combining this impedance value with your age, height, gender, and weight, the Galaxy Watch calculates your body composition with surprising accuracy.

Safety Warning

Do NOT use the BIA sensor if you have an implanted pacemaker, cardiac defibrillator, or any other life-sustaining medical device. The microcurrent, though weak, could interfere with pacemaker operations. Additionally, pregnant users should avoid BIA measurements, as tissue changes during pregnancy can skew accuracy.

How to Ensure an Accurate Measurement

Because BIA relies on electrical signals and water conductivity, your body's hydration levels heavily influence the results. To ensure your readings are consistent over time, follow these guidelines:

  1. Measure at the Same Time: Measure in the morning, after using the restroom but before eating breakfast, drinking water, or exercising. This minimizes temporary water weight fluctuations.
  2. Clean the Sensor: Wipe the back sensor and the two physical side keys of the watch with a clean cloth to remove sweat, oil, or dust.
  3. Check Your Posture: Stand up, raise your arms slightly away from your torso, and ensure your hands do not touch each other. If your arms touch your sides, the current will take a shortcut, skipping your upper body and skewing the measurement.

Step-by-Step Measurement Guide

Once you are ready, follow these steps to take your body composition reading:

  • Open the Samsung Health app on your Galaxy Watch.
  • Scroll down and tap Body composition.
  • Tap Measure.
  • Enter your current body weight (it is essential to enter an accurate weight, as the calculations rely on it).
  • Slide the watch slightly up your wrist, away from the wrist bone, and ensure it fits snugly.
  • Place your middle finger on the top button (Home key) and your ring finger on the bottom button (Back key). Do not let your fingers touch your wrist or the other hand.
  • Keep still, raise your arms, and hold the position for about 15 seconds. The watch will display a progress circle and beep when finished.

Understanding the Health Metrics

Once the measurement completes, Samsung Health generates several metrics. Here is what they mean:

Metric What it Measures Ideal Goal Trend
Skeletal Muscle Mass (SMM) The weight of the muscles you can actively train (excludes cardiac/smooth muscles). Increase (indicates strength gains).
Fat Mass The actual weight of fat tissue in your body. Decrease (for weight loss and cardiovascular wellness).
Body Fat Percentage (PBF) The proportion of fat compared to total body weight. 10-20% for men, 18-28% for women (general fitness).
Body Water The total volume of fluid in your body. Stable (50-65% depending on muscle mass).
BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate) The number of calories your body burns at complete rest to stay alive. Increase (more muscle increases BMR, helping burn calories passively).

Smartwatch BIA vs. Smart Scales vs. DEXA Scan

How does the Galaxy Watch BIA sensor compare to other measurement methods? A clinical DEXA (dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry) scan remains the gold standard, offering 98% accuracy, but it is expensive and requires a clinic visit. Smart scales use a foot-to-foot BIA current, which primarily measures your lower body. The Galaxy Watch uses a hand-to-hand BIA current, focusing on your upper body.

Studies have shown that the Galaxy Watch's BIA sensor has a strong correlation (about 70-80% accuracy) with DEXA scans, making it a fantastic tool for tracking relative trends over time, even if the absolute decimal numbers differ slightly from medical lab equipment.

Stop stressing over the scale weight. By regularly measuring your body composition on your Galaxy Watch under consistent conditions, you can accurately track muscle gain and fat loss, ensuring your diet and workouts are moving you in the right direction.
